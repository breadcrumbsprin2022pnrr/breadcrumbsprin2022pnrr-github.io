Detailed description of the project



Research objectives

In this project, we focus our investigation on Urban Air Mobility (UAM). We consider missions in the Specific category with the additional low-altitude constraint enforced by the Open category (i.e., within 120 m) ignoring the manned-unmanned risk. We concentrate our attention on two types of missions: i) suburban missions: UAVs fly in areas loosely populated for collecting images/data or for performing aerial work such as pest monitoring or data collection on orchards, or monitoring natural or artificial corridors like rivers, electricity, and gas pipelines; and ii) urban missions: UAVs fly in cluttered areas, medium or highly populated, for structural health monitoring of buildings, traffic monitoring on train railways and highways, and delivery of food and beverage, small parcel mails, or biomedical goods. Reliable BVLoS links are thought to extend the operative radius of the UAV service in both types of missions, which have a different level of risk: the suburban missions have generally negligible air and ground risk, while the urban missions face much higher air and ground risks. For our scope, we need novel enabling technologies to implement a robust BVLoS flight that guarantees a degree of connectivity and incorporates a risk tolerance at a proper level, commensurate with the offered service.





The connectivity is needed to control the UAV beyond the horizon of the operator’s vision and beyond the Ground Control Station’s (GCS) transmission range. We are not thinking of creating an ad-hoc infrastructure for letting the UAVs fly in BVLoS because it would have a negative environmental impact, and would be extremely costly. Therefore, we aim to exploit the available wireless communication technology used for other common purposes, like the cellular infrastructure that is regularly used by billions of Internet of Things (IoT) devices. In case a singular cellular infrastructure does not fully cover the entire aerial space (nor to certify the coverage in certain areas), we plan to rely on a secondary (redundant) technology (as a backup), such as the satellite infrastructure. Since these communication infrastructures (cellular and satellite) comprise different generations of technologies, each of them with different coverage, bandwidth, reliability, latency, and so on, our objective is to deal with these similar but heterogeneous technologies/interfaces in order to guarantee a seamless BVLoS flight of UAVs. Nevertheless, there are other challenges to be addressed when studying connectivity. In fact, since the UAVs will be equipped with many additional wireless transceivers increasing their weight and CPU burden, the flight time will be negatively affected. Moreover, even if the ground infrastructure is potentially suitable for the UAVs, current exceptional events can cause a saturation of the network, and hence increase latency or, even worse, possible BVLoS links disruptions. To this end, we propose a robust connectivity analysis by taking into account the communication channel quality and the required constraints.

The risk awareness helps the UAVs to be aware in advance of the possible ground and air risks. So, our goal is to build the UAV risk awareness by merging real-time information with the information stored in ground and air maps, static or dynamic. It will be of different levels by relying on onboard sensors and on the UAV control technology. The onboard risk awareness is implemented by equipping the UAVs with a set of visual and proximity sensors so that they can measure and estimate in real-time the distance between any object in the surrounding area. It can be also enhanced with the feedback provided by possible additional cameras (e.g., RGB, thermal). The UAV control technology, instead, leveraging on ground maps, limits the risks of the UAV flight. For instance, it can inform the UAV well in advance, with respect to the onboard sensors, about the presence of static obstacles, no-fly zones, and so on. The maps that describe and constrain specific areas on the ground/air can be retrieved from many providers such as OpenStreetMap or D-Flight. The content of the maps can be queried by the control infrastructures to guide the UAV during the flight. We also propose an advanced risk awareness by considering the dynamic aspects of the environment, possibly requiring communications from different entities operating in the environment during the UAV flight.

So, the important objectives to be pursued in this project are the implementation of seamless, efficient, and reliable BVLoS links, and the creation of a model that properly builds the risk awareness for the UAV by extracting data from the onboard sensors, and from the information provided by the UAV control technology.

In the following, we describe how we intend to split our project into milestones (MLs), tasks (Ts), and deliverables (Ds). ML1 concerns general activities that span the whole project, while ML2 investigates the preliminary BVLoS in suburban missions in order to lay the foundations for the subsequent and more challenging BVLoS in urban missions (ML3). POLITO will address concerns about the connectivity required during the flight (i.e., communication channel quality, constraints, and strategies for optimizing the QoS), UNIFI will study the possible risks that can occur to UAVs (i.e., safety risk assessment process and security and safety robustness UAV system evaluation), and finally, UNIPG will devise efficient routing and scheduling algorithms taking into account both the connectivity and the risk assessment, possibly extended to multiple UAVs.



ML1: General Activities

This milestone, which spans the whole project, is used to describe general activities that will support the core of our research, such as the study of the state-of-the-art, implementation and evaluation, and management, dissemination, and exploitation.



T1.1 – [UNIPG, UNIFI, POLITO; B1-B2, B6-B7] State-of-the-art analysis and requirements

This task will start to analyze the current state-of-the-art. All the RUs will do this task in order to build the required knowledge for the next milestones/activities. Concerning the communications, for each type of available network (different generations of cellular communications), we will analyze its range, distribution, bandwidth, latency, energy, and overhead cost. We will also analyze satellite communications in the case that both cellular and satellite infrastructures will be jointly used. As for the risk model, we will revise the literature on ground and air risk. For example, we must revise how the risks are assessed, how the ground information is freely available, and how malicious devices can somehow deceive UAVs during a BVLoS flight. Finally, concerning the routing of drones, we should investigate the key parameters to be optimized during the flight (energy, overhead, quality of the signal). For each area of the project (communications, risks, and routing), at the end of this task, we will propose a set of key functional requirements for the suburban and urban missions, respectively, that will drive the design of the BVLoS solution proposed by this project.



T1.2 – [UNIPG, UNIFI, POLITO; B4-B6, B10-B12] Implementation and evaluation

In this task, we implement our proposed methodologies (models, analysis, algorithms), and then evaluate the real effectiveness. Specifically, we consider first the implementation of BVLoS flights in suburban scenarios, and then in the urban scenarios. The algorithms will be designed and evaluated from a theoretical point of view, comparing them against state-of-the-art techniques. We also plan to validate the theoretical results obtained in our research project and the proposed approaches through experiments in simulated and real-world environments. Experiments can raise new conjectures and theoretical questions that may lead to further theoretical improvements and eventually to more solutions. All the RUs will do this task. Initially, each RU will focus on the implementation/assessment and evaluation of its specific area, and then we expect to convey our findings in order to evaluate the final effectiveness of the whole proposed BVLoS system.



T1.3 – [UNIPG, UNIFI, POLITO; B4-B12] Management, dissemination, and exploitation

In this task, we plan a suitable management, dissemination, and exploitation scheme. The management will be organized by the lead RU (UNIPG) which will follow up on deliverables and milestones. The lead RU will monitor the progress and identify roadblocks, make sure all deliverables have been met, and finalize the project. The goal is also to cover both scientific dissemination and exploitation of the project’s objectives, adopted approaches, and of obtained results. The effort will be allocated to document demonstrators, research prototypes, and provide open-source code and other supporting materials. A project web space will be created to provide an information point for the work of the project. The project results, research findings, and outcomes will be publicly disseminated through seminar talks, and also published in highly reputable international scientific journals and conferences, possibly under the open-access option. In particular, we expect a productive scientific dissemination, which can be quantified in 6 top-rank journal papers and 6 conference papers (on average, 3 journal and 3 conference papers per year). Additionally, at least 2 journal publications will be co-authored by ALL the RUs, which describes the proposed approach as a cohesive work, where all RUs have a fundamental role in its success. To promote the dissemination of results to the community, two dissemination events will be organized, in the form of workshops, possibly co-located in top conferences. Internally, we will have a joint secure cloud space for several services, i.e., source code repository, storage, web space, and so on.



ML2: BVLoS in Suburban Missions

In this milestone, our goal is to enable Specific BVLoS suburban missions for collecting images/data or for performing aerial work such as spraying on agriculture, or monitoring natural or artificial corridors like rivers, electricity, and gas pipelines, just to mention a few. In particular, our target will be pursued by: analyzing the link quality and constraints of the suburban scenario and characterizing the relationship between the cellular networks on the ground, and the UAVs (T2.1); analyzing the risk of UAVs by relying on a properly modeled safety risk assessment process (T2.2); and defining the routing parameters to be optimized for an efficient flight that considers both the connectivity and risk assessment processes (T2.3). Eventually, we expect to produce Deliverable D2 that summarizes the activities expected for BVLoS flights in suburban missions describing our findings, results, implementations, and simulation evaluation.



T2.1 – [POLITO; B2-B6] Communication channel quality and constraints

In this task, we define the communication channel quality and constraints, which is a mandatory step for enabling the BVLoS flight. This will be done by taking into account the existing communication infrastructures, starting from the already available cellular ground infrastructure (i.e., 2/3/4/5G). This infrastructure, mainly optimized for ground users, is basically made by a network of cellular towers whose antennas are placed at a certain height above the ground. Since these antennas are directional and they are pointing towards the ground, the connectivity can be an issue in the air. The investigation consists in defining many types of aspects, starting from the channel quality in terms of key performance indicators like bit rate, and error probability. We also need to study possible additional constraints, like the maximum distance between the transmitter and the receiver, the time to handover, the expected number of handovers per unit of covered distance (which depends on the cell size), and so on. Furthermore, we propose models of the cost (in terms of energy and latency) that characterize possible handovers among intra- and inter-technologies. The selected models will have to be simple in order to be easily incorporated into decision routing algorithms and strategies that will optimize the flight of UAVs. Moreover, we also plan to evaluate possible signal noises caused by the non-obstructed environment at relatively low altitudes (i.e., suburban areas). This is because the current cellular infrastructure has been built for an environment with obstacles. Therefore, since the drones fly over the buildings, there is not any obstruction, and hence the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is increased in the nearby towers.



T2.2 – [UNIFI; B2-B6] Safety risk assessment process

In this task, we provide an adaptable modeling framework for safety analyses, focusing on BVLoS operations, that allows the assurance of operational safety. Hence, we model the accidental faults that involve the UAV itself, including the connectivity disruptions. In particular, as for the UAV failures, key model parameters, like those abstracting the failure rate of a hardware component, are usually meant to be static. In this case, instead, they have to be adapted to a dynamic environment where the environment conditions (e.g., weather, UAV velocity) can influence the failure rate. A hardware component failure impacts the ground risk, while the communication link failure exposes the UAV control to be risky. The key elements of the proposed modeling approach concern its capability to capture the dynamic nature of the system elements that may have an impact on the offered level of safety, and its capability to be applied to a variety of scenarios. The adaptable modeling framework will be based on a preliminary risk assessment process encompassing safety issues. We will first conduct a qualitative analysis for identifying the safety hazards in the system that might lead to catastrophic consequences. Moreover, a quantitative analysis can start to define which failures can be the object of resilient solutions. For example, having an uncontrollable UAV is generally unacceptable, but a connectivity outage can be tolerated by resilient solutions in a suburban environment if it is “short enough”. Traditional risk assessment techniques, like hazard and operability study (HAZOP) or failure mode and effects analysis (FMEA) (i.e., the process of reviewing as many components, assemblies, and subsystems as possible to identify potential failure modes in a system and their causes and effects), will be tailored for being effectively applied to the BVLoS domain.



T2.3 – [UNIPG; B2-B6] Routing algorithms for BVLoS in suburban mission

In this task, we initially create the system model which is at the basis of our BVLoS scenario. It should take into account both the mobility of the drone and some detailed information about the environment that surrounds the flights. So, we aim to design a system model based on a multi-layer framework in which each layer contains useful data, such as coverage of the communication towers, obstacles, weather maps, rechargeable stations, no-fly zones, and ground risks. Thus, the objective is to devise efficient routing algorithms that allow the UAVs to fly BVLoS in suburban missions by taking into account the connectivity and the risk assessment process. In other words, given a starting and a destination point, we need to determine the “most suitable” BVLoS route such that the UAV can autonomously reach its destination, optimizing metrics that measure and balance sustainability, efficiency, connectivity, and risk. If the time complexity for getting the optimal solutions would be too high, we will target sub-optimal but faster approximated (and heuristic) solutions. We also plan to take possible countermeasures in the case the UAVs enter “fail-safe mode”, providing a set of “checkpoints” along the route where the drone can land, whenever the self-diagnostic system detects a malfunction, but still within functioning capabilities. To improve the resilience of the connectivity, we can assume drones are forced to be connected to at least two different types of links (e.g., 2G and 5G). Having this redundancy is important in order to always guarantee communications with the drone, although with different network capabilities. Finally, the routing algorithms will be tuned with respect to the application of interest. For instance, if the drone is recording and transmitting a video in livestreaming then it will need to maintain excellent communication at any time, without interfering too much with the other users. Conversely, in package delivery applications, only a few data need to be exchanged with the GCS.



ML3: BVLoS in Urban Missions

In this milestone, based on the new acquired knowledge, our goal is to investigate the Specific BVLoS urban missions, e.g., for structural health monitoring of buildings, traffic monitoring on highways, monitoring of railway tracks, and delivery of food and beverage, small parcel mail, and biomedical goods, just to mention a few. In particular, our target will be pursued by: developing strategies for optimizing the Quality of Service (QoS) of the communications (T3.1); evaluating the security and safety robustness of the UAV system against accidental and malicious faults (T3.2); and devising advanced UAV routing and scheduling algorithms (T3.3). Eventually, we expect to produce Deliverable D3 that summarizes the activities expected for BVLoS flights in urban missions.



T3.1 – [POLITO; B6-B12] Development of strategies for optimizing the QoS

In this task, we develop possible strategies for optimizing the QoS provided to the application taking into account the channel quality and the predicted channel quality along the path that the UAV is taking. In fact, a UAV that is flying towards a given destination by accessing different technological solutions can optimize the path selection not only to guarantee continuity of connection but also to maximize the QoS that is required by the applications. In particular, we will consider a few cases as follows: i) in case of elastic and delay tolerant services, the strategy aims at maximizing the available bandwidth along the path that the UAV is taking; ii) in case of multimedia real-time communications, it might be convenient to guarantee an available bandwidth that is as constant as possible and as close as possible to the minimum requested one; iii) in case of energy minimization, for example for IoT applications, the scheduling of the transmissions might take into account the fact that the energy needed to transmit depends on the channel quality and on the selected technology. Another aspect to consider in urban environments is the presence of dense obstacles that can obstruct the signal and therefore limit the connectivity. Recall that a UAV must be connected all the time, with a certain level of requirements, during any BVLoS flight. In fact, in a suburban scenario with a few obstacles, the probability of being radio Line of Sight (LoS) is higher, but in an urban scenario, this probability can significantly decrease due to the presence of buildings or other obstacles. Such a LoS probability highly depends on the angle of incidence among the tower where the UAV is connected with, and the UAV itself, impacting the required QoS.



T3.2 – [UNIFI; B6-B12] Security and safety robustness UAV system evaluation

In this task, we consider security and safety issues with the most stringent constraints because the environment on the ground is dense and highly populated, and the buildings form a cluttered environment. All the previous concerns addressed in T2.2 are present here, and the models have to be adapted to the cluttered environment. In addition, the complicated and uncontrolled environments, the different wireless communication channels, and the lack of security standards for UAVs make the Unmanned Aerial System (UAS) vulnerable to attack. UAVs/UAS are a kind of cyber-physical system (CPS) containing multiple components like sensors, communications, computation, and control modules, so each element may become the object of cyber-attacks, leading the physical system into undesirable states and critical malfunctions that could lead to potential catastrophic consequences, thus impairing the system safety. So, it is necessary to analyze the cyber-physical threats and the impact of cross-domain attacks on UAVs, which is also an important research direction in the field of CPS security. We plan to provide the information required to experimentally evaluate the robustness of the UAS against accidental and malicious faults. Starting from the most likely fault modes and the most relevant attack surfaces (exploiting UAV’s vulnerabilities), we will investigate the operating condition of the UAV at its operational boundary, i.e., we will investigate if the UAV is still able to provide a correct service under the considered accidental faults and attacks. The analysis will include failures of the visual camera, the gyroscope, the oscillometer, and so on. As attacks, we will include at least adversarial patches (i.e., attacks towards visual cameras in the physical world) and disruption of wireless communications. When the injection of failures and attacks directly into the real prototype is not possible, simulations will be performed using the available datasets and simulators. Also, the presence of multiple drones in the same area can cause additional risks (e.g., colliding drones). So, the proposed approach seeks to begin providing an adaptable framework for analyses that allows rapid assurance of operational safety considering different scenarios in terms of combinations of the variability aspects of the system, like combinations of environment conditions, operational conditions, available communication networks, number of UAVs.



T3.3 – [UNIPG; B6-B12] Routing Algorithms for BVLoS in urban mission

In this task, we study efficient routing and scheduling algorithms suitable for an urban BVLoS environment. Differently from the previous suburban context of T2.3, here the algorithms should also take into account the ground characteristics, extending the multi-layer framework with, for example, the layer of the population density and the sheltering factor, as well as a layer for dynamic events. The routing algorithms will consider the strategies proposed in T3.1 (to adapt the QoS to the applications) and in T3.2 (for a reliable and resilient flight). In fact, due to the dynamicity of the events, the risk layers can even change over time (e.g., in localized fairs the concentration of people can significantly increase) or in case of exceptional events (e.g., earthquakes or tornadoes that create new obstacles). Therefore, these algorithms must also be able to recompute the drone’s path on-the-fly. In an urban environment, we also expect to deal with multiple drones that perform different tasks at the same time. This aspect introduces the problem that two or more UAVs can collide together during their flight. Therefore, we need to study and devise efficient and resilient scheduling algorithms in order to guarantee that multiple UAVs do not fly in the same area or the same corridor. An approach to solve this problem can consider the tessellation of the 3D space in cubes, and then assign a cube to each drone with some policies. Along corridors the strategy could be to let the drones fly at different heights, avoiding so to occupy the same horizontal line. However, in the extreme case that too many UAVs are flying in the same area, they should be opportunely re-routed on different, and possibly longer, paths, satisfying the required application constraints. This would introduce feasibility issues since UAVs are energy-constrained, and hence the energy required for the new path could not be sufficient. If this happens, the drones should be able to perform a safe landing on intermediate checkpoints. Methodologically, whenever possible, we will model the problems that we face as optimization problems. We will study their computational complexity, and if they are tractable, we will propose optimal solutions, otherwise, approximated solutions and heuristics will be devised.
